The London to Kent Move: Five Years On - Is It Worth It?

Published by Relokates Removals · 2027-01-12

Thousands of Londoners have made the move to Kent over the past decade, accelerated by the pandemic shift to flexible working and the relentless pressure on London property prices. Five years on from the peak of the London-to-Kent movement, what do people actually think? We have spoken to clients who made the move and here is the honest picture.

What they love

Space is the most consistently mentioned benefit. A London flat that felt adequate becomes visibly inadequate once you have lived in a Kent house with a garden for six months. The garden in particular - something that most London buyers could not previously afford - becomes central to daily life in a way that surprises almost everyone.

The pace is the second most mentioned benefit. Not slow - Kent towns like Sevenoaks, Tonbridge and Canterbury have genuine vitality. But a different pace. The school run is different. The supermarket is different. The sense of urgency that London generates constantly is absent, and most people find they do not miss it.

What surprised them

The car dependency. London living is built around public transport. Kent living outside the main commuter towns requires a car for almost everything. Families who moved to rural Kent villages particularly noticed this - children's activities, grocery shopping and social life all require driving.

The London visits. Most movers expected to return to London regularly for work, friends and culture. In practice, those visits become less frequent than expected - not because Kent is lacking, but because London feels more distant psychologically once you have left than the train time suggests.

What they miss

Food diversity. London's restaurant and food culture is genuinely unmatched. Kent has improved significantly but the range available within walking distance of a Kent home is not comparable to London.

The walk. Urban walkability - being able to reach shops, cafes, parks and friends on foot - is something people do not fully value until it is gone.

Would they do it again?

The consistent answer from clients we have spoken to is yes - with the caveat that managing expectations about what Kent is and is not makes the transition easier. It is not London with more space. It is a different way of living that most people, five years on, would not trade back.
